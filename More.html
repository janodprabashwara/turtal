Introduction

Sri Lanka, a beautiful island nation located in the Indian Ocean, is renowned for its rich biodiversity and diverse ecosystems.
One of the most fascinating aspects of Sri Lanka's wildlife is its turtle population.
The country's pristine beaches serve as nesting grounds for several species of sea turtles, making Sri Lanka a significant global hotspot for turtle conservation and research.


Sri Lanka is home to five species of sea turtles, all of which are endangered or critically endangered. These species include the Green turtle (Chelonia mydas),
Loggerhead turtle (Caretta caretta), Hawksbill turtle (Eretmochelys imbricata), Olive Ridley turtle (Lepidochelys olivacea), and Leatherback turtle (Dermochelys coriacea).
Each species has its unique characteristics, nesting behavior, and ecological significance.


Sri Lanka's turtle population also attracts tourists from around the world, offering unique opportunities to witness these magnificent creatures up close.
Responsible turtle tourism provides both economic benefits to local communities and educational experiences for visitors, promoting awareness and conservation efforts.

Our Mission & Vision

Our Mission

To protect and conserve sea turtles through research,
rehabilitation, and education, ensuring their survival for future generations.
We are committed to safeguarding sea turtles by addressing the various threats they face, including habitat degradation, pollution, and unsustainable fishing practices.

Our Vision

We envision a world where sea turtles thrive in their natural habitats, free from threats and endangerment. Through our dedicated efforts, we strive to create awareness, engage communities, and implement effective conservation strategies that support the long-term survival of sea turtles.

Protect SriLankan Turtles

  • Restricting human interference: Strict regulations are implemented to limit human activities near nesting beaches and hatcheries to minimize disturbance to the turtles.

  • Nest relocation: In areas where nests are at risk of being washed away or damaged by natural elements or predators, hatchery staff may relocate the eggs to safer locations.

  • Protection from predators: Measures are taken to prevent predatory animals, such as dogs and monitor lizards, from accessing the nests or hatchlings.

  • Educating local communities: Awareness programs are conducted to educate local communities about the importance of turtle conservation and the need to protect the hatcheries.

  • Monitoring and research: Regular monitoring of nesting sites and hatcheries helps track the number of nests, hatching success rates, and the health of the turtles, enabling better conservation strategies.

  • Conservation laws and regulations: The Sri Lankan government has established laws and regulations to protect sea turtles and their nesting habitats, making it illegal to harm or disturb these endangered creatures.
